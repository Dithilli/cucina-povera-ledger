## The big one — *Sin maíz no hay país* ("without corn, there is no country")

Mexican food is the longest-running frugality experiment on the planet — about
**nine thousand years** of feeding people on corn, beans, chilies, and squash,
and making it taste like a celebration. Long before there was a Mexico, the
peoples of Mesoamerica built a complete civilization on a single, brilliant
agricultural idea — and then a single, brilliant kitchen-chemistry one.

The idea is the **milpa**: not a field of one crop but a polyculture where corn,
beans, and squash grow together — the **Three Sisters**. Corn gives the beans a
pole to climb; beans fix nitrogen back into the soil; squash shades out weeds.
And on the plate, the same trio does something quietly miraculous. **Corn lacks
the amino acids lysine and tryptophan; beans have them in abundance but lack
methionine, which corn supplies.** Eaten together — a tortilla and a spoon of
beans — they form a **complete protein**, nutritionally close to meat, for
pennies. Indigenous farmers worked this out by observation millennia before
biochemistry named it.

The chemistry is **nixtamalization**: soaking and cooking dried corn in an
alkaline solution of lime or wood ash. Discovered around 1500 BCE, it does what
looks like sorcery — it releases the corn's bound niacin (preventing the
deficiency disease *pellagra* that later devastated corn-eating Europeans who
skipped this step), softens the grain so it can be ground into **masa**, and
deepens its flavor and aroma. Every tortilla, tamal, and bowl of pozole descends
from this 3,500-year-old act of bio-engineering. **Corn was so central it was
sacred:** in the Maya *Popol Vuh*, the gods make the first humans out of maize.
People made of corn, eating corn — the cuisine never forgot it.

Then came 1521 and the *encuentro*. The Spanish brought pork, lard, chicken,
cheese, onions, garlic, citrus, rice, cinnamon; what grew from the collision is
**mestizo** cooking — Old World ingredients folded into an unbroken Indigenous
grammar of corn, chile, and tomato. Crucially, the expensive imports became
**seasonings, not centers**: a little lard to enrich the beans, a fistful of
shredded chicken to fill a taco, a crumble of *queso fresco* on top. Meat
stretches; the milpa carries.

The everyday economy of this food is the **fonda** and the **mercado** — the
market stall and the family kitchen — where the daily *comida corrida* feeds a
worker a full meal for almost nothing, and where **`no se tira nada`** ("nothing
is thrown away") rules as firmly as it does in any Italian *nonna*'s kitchen.
Stale tortillas become *chilaquiles* and *sopa de tortilla*; yesterday's beans
become *refritos*; bones become *caldo*; the whole squash plant — fruit, blossom,
vine — goes in the pot.

This challenge is about cooking the way most Mexicans actually have for nine
millennia: **corn and beans at the center, chile doing the work that money can't,
nothing wasted, and a feast made from almost nothing.** The constraint isn't a
hardship here — it's the entire reason the food is this good.

## The little ones — week by week

### Week 1 — Masa (the tortilla & the *comal*)
Everything starts here, on the *comal* (the flat griddle). Nixtamalized corn,
ground to masa, pressed and cooked into the **tortilla** — the plate, the utensil,
and the bread of Mesoamerica all at once. This week is the foundation: tortillas,
*quesadillas*, *tlacoyos* (masa pockets stuffed with beans), *sopes*, *memelas*.
Learn the masa and you've learned the cuisine. **Protein:** masa + a smear of
beans + crumbled *queso fresco* already starts stacking the complete-protein math.
**Precious thread:** good melting cheese (*Oaxaca* or *quesillo*).
**Zero-waste hero:** *chilaquiles* — yesterday's stale tortillas, fried and
simmered in salsa, crowned with an egg.

### Week 2 — Frijoles (the bean pot)
The other half of the sacred pair, and the protein engine of the whole challenge.
A pot of **frijoles de la olla** — dried beans simmered slow with onion and epazote
— costs about a dollar and gives roughly **12–15g of protein per cup**, the
cheapest near-complete protein there is (especially eaten with corn). This week:
*frijoles de la olla*, *refritos* (refried in a little lard — the one place the
fat earns its keep), *frijoles charros* (cowboy beans, brothy with scraps of
pork). The bean pot is your bank; it pays out all week. **Precious thread:** a
little lard or a knuckle of bacon to enrich the pot. **Zero-waste hero:** day-two
beans mashed into *refritos*; the bean broth becomes a soup base.

### Week 3 — Chiles & salsas (the flavor system)
This is the secret of how the poor ate *well* and not just enough. **Chile** —
fresh and dried — is the engine of flavor that turns a plain tortilla and beans
into something you crave. Toast dried chiles (*guajillo, ancho, chile de árbol,
pasilla*), char tomatoes and tomatillos, grind them in the *molcajete* (volcanic
mortar): *salsa roja*, *salsa verde*, *salsa macha*. Almost free, infinitely
variable, and the difference between subsistence and joy. **Protein:** chiles
carry the *legume/egg* base of other weeks. **Precious thread:** a small bag of
good dried chiles. **Zero-waste hero:** *salsa* is itself the anti-waste tool —
it makes any leftover delicious again.

### Week 4 — Huevos (the everyday protein)
The cheap protein bridge, exactly as eggs are in the Italian arc. The
backyard hen feeds the Mexican kitchen too: **huevos a la mexicana** (scrambled
with tomato, onion, chile), **huevos rancheros** (fried eggs over tortilla and
salsa), **huevos en salsa**, **machacado** (eggs with dried beef). With beans and
a tortilla, an egg breakfast clears the protein floor before the day's even
started. **Precious thread:** a little *crema* or *chorizo* to gild it.
**Zero-waste hero:** *migas* — stale tortilla chips scrambled into the eggs.

### Week 5 — Maíz más allá de la tortilla (corn in its other forms)
Corn is not only the tortilla. This week explores its other lives:
**pozole** (nixtamalized corn cooked whole into a great celebratory hominy stew),
**esquites** and *elote* (street corn off the cob and on), **tamales** (masa
steamed in a husk — the original portable meal), **atole** (the warm corn drink).
Same sacred grain, a half-dozen transformations. **Protein:** pozole stretches a
little meat across a huge pot; tamales carry beans or cheese. **Precious thread:**
a small amount of pork or chicken for the pozole pot. **Zero-waste hero:** corn
husks, cobs (cob broth!), and the masa scrapings all get used.

### Week 6 — Verduras y la milpa (the garden & the greens)
The third sister and her companions — the vegetable week, the most direct taste
of the milpa. **Nopales** (cactus paddles, nearly free and full of fiber),
**calabacita** (squash) and its **flor de calabaza** (blossom), **quelites**
(wild greens — *verdolagas/*purslane, *huauzontle*), **rajas** (roasted chile
strips with cream and corn). This is eating the whole plant and the whole season,
*acelgas*-to-blossoms. **Protein:** paired with beans, eggs, or a little cheese.
**Precious thread:** *crema* and *queso fresco* for the rajas and squash.
**Zero-waste hero:** *sopa de guías* / *calabacitas* — squash fruit, blossom, and
vine in one pot, nothing of the plant wasted.

### Week 7 — Guisos (the Sunday pot & the finale)
The celebratory but still-frugal stew — how the poor ate like kings on Sundays by
**stretching** a little meat through a big pot. **Tinga** (shredded chicken in a
smoky chipotle-tomato sauce), **picadillo** (a little ground meat bulked with
potato, carrot, raisins), **mole** in its everyday forms, **frijoles puercos**.
One affordable protein feeds many, ladled over rice or scooped with tortillas —
the communal table, the reward at the end of the arc. **Protein:** meat as a
flavor-carrier over the bean-and-corn base. **Precious thread:** the week's one
cut of meat, plus *Mexican chocolate* if you build a mole. **Zero-waste hero:**
the guiso *is* the technique for using up odds and ends; leftovers become taco and
*torta* fillings.
