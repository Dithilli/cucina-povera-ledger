## The big one — *La cucina povera*

Italian food, as the rest of the world fell in love with it, is mostly **poor
people's food in a nice shirt.** *Cucina povera* — "the kitchen of the poor" —
is the cooking of the *contadini*, the peasant farmers and laborers who for
centuries had land but little money, and who turned that scarcity into one of the
most imitated cuisines on earth. It is not a style someone invented; it is what
happened when people with almost nothing refused to eat badly.

Its governing law is **`non si butta via niente`** — you throw nothing away.
Stale bread is not waste; it is tomorrow's *ribollita*, *panzanella*,
*pappa al pomodoro*. The pasta water is a sauce ingredient. The rind of the
cheese goes into the soup. This is the *arte di arrangiarsi* — the art of making
do, of arranging something out of not-much — and it is closer to a moral
philosophy than a set of recipes.

The cuisine is fiercely **regional**, because poverty was local: you ate what
your patch of Italy actually grew. The South lived on **dried pasta**, tomatoes,
chilies, and a thread of olive oil — *pastasciutta*, the democratic plate. **Tuscany**
canonized **stale bread** into a dozen dishes and baked its loaves without salt
(*pane sciocco*) so they'd pair with salty cured pork. The **North** ate
**polenta** — golden, filling, and, in a dark historical irony, the cause of
mass *pellagra*, because Italians never learned the alkali step (nixtamalization)
that Mesoamericans used on the very same grain to unlock its niacin. The same
corn, two continents, opposite fates — a reminder that frugal genius is hard-won,
not automatic.

What unites the regions is a **structure of restriction** that the rich never
needed: a cheap **starch** (pasta, bread, polenta, rice) carries the plate;
**legumes and eggs** do the protein work that meat couldn't afford to; a few
**`odori`** — garlic, onion, herbs, a salted anchovy, a knuckle of pecorino —
do the flavor work; and one small **luxury** per week (good oil, aged cheese,
a little fish) is the thread of gold through the cloth. Meat was Sunday, or a
seasoning, never the center.

The point of this challenge is to cook the way they cooked — beautifully, inside
hard limits, wasting nothing — and to discover what they knew: **the constraint
is the creativity.** You are not depriving yourself. You are doing historical
re-enactment with a scoreboard.

## The little ones — week by week

### Week 1 — Pasta
The Southern heartbeat. For the poor of Naples and the *Mezzogiorno*, dried pasta
was the great equalizer — shelf-stable, filling, and able to carry a "sauce" made
from literally nothing: garlic and oil (*aglio e olio*), a crushed tomato,
chickpeas and their broth (*pasta e ceci*), toasted breadcrumbs standing in for
cheese (*la mollica*, "the poor man's parmesan"). The art is in the *sugo* and
the salted water. This week sets the pattern for everything that follows: a cheap
starch made glorious by technique, not money.

### Week 2 — Bread
Tuscany's monument to thrift. The region's saltless loaf (*pane sciocco*) goes
stale fast, so an entire cuisine grew up to resurrect it: *ribollita* ("reboiled"
— bean-and-kale soup thickened with yesterday's bread and *better* the next day),
*panzanella* (bread salad), *pappa al pomodoro* (bread-and-tomato porridge),
*acquacotta* ("cooked water," finished with an egg). Nothing here is a recipe so
much as a refusal to waste. This is `non si butta via niente` at its purest.

### Week 3 — Eggs
The *cortile* economy — the farmyard hen. Eggs were the everyday protein the poor
could actually keep producing, and the *frittata* is the great catch-all: whatever
greens, scraps, or leftover pasta you have, bound with egg and made dinner. After
the carbohydrate weeks, this is the recovery week — the protein floor is met
almost without trying. Cheap, fast, and forgiving.

### Week 4 — Legumes
The Tuscans were mocked as *mangiafagioli* — "bean-eaters" — and wore it proudly.
Dried beans, lentils, and chickpeas were the meat of the poor: stored all winter
in the cellar, simmered slow with sage and a little oil, eaten with bread or
pasta. This is the cheapest protein-and-calories that exists, so it posts your
best cost-per-100g of the whole challenge. Lentils at New Year for luck; beans
*all'uccelletto* any day at all.

### Week 5 — Polenta
The Northern staple — Veneto, Lombardy, Friuli. For the rural poor of the North,
golden cornmeal mush *was* dinner, often all there was. Stir it soft with a
little cheese (*polenta concia*), let it set and grill it, or spoon a few beans
or a mushroom ragù over it. It carries a hard lesson too: relying on un-treated
corn gave the Northern poor *pellagra* for generations — frugality without
knowledge can wound. Eaten well, with a legume or an egg alongside, it's pure
comfort.

### Bonus — Sicily
The island apart, where Arab, Greek, and Spanish rule layered onto peasant
scarcity to produce something singular. The sea gives cheap oily fish (sardines,
anchovies); the land gives eggplant, wild fennel, capers, and a love of
*agrodolce* (sweet-sour). *Pasta con le sarde* (sardines, wild fennel, raisins,
pine nuts, breadcrumbs), *caponata* (the sweet-sour eggplant relic of Arab
Sicily), *sarde a beccafico* — poverty cooking dressed in a thousand years of
conquest.

### Bonus — The Garden (*l'orto*)
The kitchen garden and the foraged hedge. When the pantry is thin, the *orto* and
the roadside provide: bitter *cicoria*, *scarola*, the whole pot of *minestrone*
built from whatever's ripe, *peperonata* in late summer. This is eating with the
season at its most literal — the week's menu is whatever the plot and the weather
decided to give you. The oldest frugality of all: eat what grows.
