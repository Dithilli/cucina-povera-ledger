## The big one — *Tsom ena Beyaynetu* ("the fast and the platter")

For up to two hundred days a year, a large share of Ethiopians and Eritreans eat
no meat, no dairy, no eggs — and eat magnificently. The Orthodox fasting calendar
is one of the strictest in the Christian world, and the cuisine it shaped is one
of the most generous: a fermented grain, a pot of legumes, and a spoon of spiced
butter, arranged into a feast that costs almost nothing. Here, **fasting and
feasting are not opposites — they are the same table.**

Orthodox Tewahedo Christians fast on most Wednesdays and Fridays, through Lent,
and across several other seasons, abstaining from all animal products. So the
everyday plate had to be built entirely from teff, lentils, peas, chickpeas, and
greens — and good enough to want on a holy day. The result is **beyaynetu** ("a
little of each"): a single round of injera laid out with small mounds of every
wot, alicha, green, and salad. It is the humble fasting meal and the celebratory
spread at once. When the fast breaks, a chicken or a little beef joins the same
platter; the structure never changes, only one mound is added.

The plate, the utensil, and the bread are one thing: **injera**, a wide, sour,
lacy flatbread made from **teff** — the tiniest grain in the world, native to the
Horn of Africa and naturally gluten-free. Teff flour is whisked with water and
left to ferment for days, until wild yeasts and bacteria turn it tangy and
bubbling, then poured thin on a hot griddle and steamed into a soft sheet covered
in "eyes" that drink up sauce. Like sourdough, the ferment is kept alive: a spoon
of yesterday's sour liquid (the *ersho*) seeds tomorrow's batch.

Because animal products were off the table so often, **legumes are the protein
engine** — red lentils (*misir*), yellow split peas (*kik*), chickpea-and-broad-bean
flour (*shiro*), favas, green lentils. A cup of cooked red lentils brings roughly
**18g of protein**; a plate of misir wot plus shiro plus kik over injera clears a
day's floor with no meat at all. Two pantry staples carry the flavor: **berbere**
(toasted dried chiles ground with a dozen warm spices) and **niter kibbeh**
(clarified butter simmered with aromatics until the milk solids brown). The
signature move is to bloom the berbere in hot niter kibbeh over a long, patient
onion sweat — the cuisine's mirepoix, and why a pot of cheap lentils tastes like a
feast.

Nothing is thrown away. Day-old injera becomes **fitfit/firfir**, **telba fitfit**
(folded into ground toasted flax), or **timatim fitfit** (tossed with a fresh
tomato salad); the offcuts toasted crisp become **kategna**. And the border between
Ethiopia and Eritrea is political, not culinary: Eritrean families keep the same
fasting calendar and the same legume table — *tsebhi birsen* is misir wot,
*tsebhi shiro* is shiro, *taita* is injera — with a Red-Sea accent of extra
fenugreek (*hilbet*) and a sizzling clay pot.

This challenge is about cooking the way most of the Horn of Africa actually has for
centuries: **a fermented grain, a pot of legumes, and a spoon of spiced butter — a
feast from almost nothing, where the fast and the feast share a platter.**

## The little ones — week by week

### Week 1 — Injera & teff (the fermented grain)
Everything starts with the ferment. **Teff** — the tiny native grain of the
highlands, naturally gluten-free — is milled, whisked with water, and left to sour
for days until it bubbles. Poured thin on a hot griddle and steamed under a lid, it
becomes **injera**, the wide, lacy, sour flatbread that is plate, utensil, and bread
at once. This week is the foundation: the ferment, the pour, the "eyes," and the
crisp-toasted offcut snacks (*kategna*, *dabo kolo*). **Protein:** the teff itself
carries a surprising amount; the legume wots that sit on top do the rest.
**Precious thread:** a bag of true ivory teff flour. **Zero-waste hero:** *kategna*
— offcut injera toasted and smeared with niter kibbeh and berbere.

### Week 2 — Misir (the red-lentil wot)
The fiery brick-red heart of the table. **Misir wot** — red lentils melted into a
silky stew over a long onion sweat and bloomed berbere — is the dish people picture
when they picture this cuisine, and its gentle twin **misir alicha** (turmeric, no
chile) is the cool counterweight. A pot of split red lentils costs almost nothing
and gives ~18g protein per cooked cup. **Protein:** the lentils alone clear most of
the day's floor. **Precious thread:** a jar of fresh-toasted berbere — the one
pantry investment that powers the whole arc. **Zero-waste hero:** leftover misir
torn into day-old injera as *misir fitfit*.

### Week 3 — Shiro (the chickpea-flour wot)
The weeknight workhorse. **Shiro** is ground roasted chickpea (or broad-bean) flour
whisked into a spiced onion base — a velvety, garlicky wot ready in fifteen minutes
from a bag of flour. So humble it was historically the only wot many families ate
outside festivals, yet so good it's the dish everyone misses from home. This week:
*shiro wot* (and the thicker clay-pot *tegabino*), *bozena shiro* (with a little
meat), and *shiro fitfit* the next morning. **Protein:** chickpea flour, cheap and
fast. **Precious thread:** a nub of niter kibbeh. **Zero-waste hero:** *shiro fitfit*
— stiffened leftover shiro and torn injera tossed together.

### Week 4 — Kik & alicha (the gentle peas)
The week the heat rests. **Yellow split peas** become sunny, turmeric-bright dishes:
*kik alicha* (whole and creamy), *ater kik alicha* (smooth purée), and their spicy
cousin *kik wot* when you want fire. The alicha dishes are the soothing, mild
counterweight that makes the fiery wots taste even better by contrast — the platter
needs both. **Protein:** split peas, ~16g per cooked cup. **Precious thread:**
*korarima* (Ethiopian cardamom) and *besobela* (sacred basil) for fragrance.
**Zero-waste hero:** any leftover alicha thinned with water becomes the next day's
soup over injera.

### Week 5 — Gomen & greens (the garden)
The restorative green week. **Gomen** (collard greens stewed soft with garlic and
ginger), **atakilt wot** (cabbage, carrot, and potato in a turmeric base),
**fasolia** (green beans and carrots), and bright cold dishes like **azifa** (a
sharp green-lentil salad) round out the legume-heavy plate with color and freshness.
This is eating the whole garden alongside the bean pot. **Protein:** greens add a
little; the day still leans on a wot or salad of lentils. **Precious thread:** a
wedge of fresh *ayib* cheese to cool the greens (*gomen be ayib*). **Zero-waste
hero:** *timatim fitfit* — a raw tomato salad tossed with yesterday's injera.

### Week 6 — Eritrean crossover (the same pot, a Red-Sea accent)
The same table, one country east. Eritrean Orthodox families keep the identical
fasting calendar and legume repertoire — *tsebhi birsen* **is** misir wot, *tsebhi
shiro* **is** shiro, *taita* **is** injera — so this week is a lesson in how a
single cuisine belongs to two nations. The accent leans toward the Red Sea: more
fenugreek, the cooling fava-and-fenugreek paste **hilbet**, and dishes brought
sizzling to the table in a clay pot. **Protein:** favas and lentils, as ever.
**Precious thread:** a handful of dried faba beans for hilbet. **Zero-waste hero:**
the shared fitfit habit crosses the border too — stale taita revived in spiced oil.

### Week 7 — Beyaynetu (the feast)
The whole arc on one platter. **Beyaynetu** ("a little of each") is the
consolidation: a great round of injera laid out with mounds of every dish from the
weeks before — misir wot, shiro, kik alicha, gomen, atakilt, a fresh salad — and,
because the fast is breaking, one indulgence: **doro wot** (chicken and eggs in
deep berbere) or **zigni** (spiced beef). One bird or a little beef feeds the whole
table, ladled beside the legumes. The fasting platter and the feast platter turn out
to be the same platter, with one mound added. **Protein:** the legumes carry it; the
meat is the seasoning luxury. **Precious thread:** one whole chicken for the doro.
**Zero-waste hero:** beyaynetu **is** the zero-waste finale — a few spoons of every
leftover wot from the week become one abundant feast.
